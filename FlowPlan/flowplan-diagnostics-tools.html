Smart Intakes - Start here · Space-constrained · upgrade scoping
Area Footprint Diagnostic
Fast screen for space-binding upgrades when the site is footprint-limited.
+
Use when
  • You’re footprint‑limited and need a fast, space‑realistic shortlist before detailed design.
  • Retrofit constraints dominate (connections, access, headloss, limited outage periods).
  • You want to test whether targets are achievable within the available space.
Inputs needed
  • Available footprint / access zones, hydraulic headloss constraints, connection points and outage periods.
  • Current flows and basic quality drivers (TSS/COD/FOG/surfactants, variability notes).
  • Utilities and practical constraints (power, chemical handling space, operator access).
Deliverables
  • Space‑efficient pathway shortlist with connection logic and layout assumptions (what must be moved/retained).
  • Key constraints that govern feasibility (headloss, access, staging, bypass needs).
  • Next-step data list to confirm the shortlist (minimum testing / trend review).
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Selecting ‘compact’ modules without connection and maintenance clearance conditions.
  • Ignoring hydraulics/headloss and creating hidden pumping/energy penalties.
KPIs & verification requirements
Footprint per m³/d (or per ML/d), headloss budget, and staging/outage feasibility. Expected operability burden (cleaning/backwash frequency, access needs). Risk flags: bypass exposure, odour hotspots, solids handling pinch points.

Smart Intakes - Start here · Objectives · performance limits
Design Intake
Establish objective, constraints and reuse intent; align on KPIs and performance limits.
+
Use when
  • You need a clean starting point: clarify the decision, constraints and verification expectations.
  • Stakeholders are misaligned (reuse intent vs budget vs operability).
  • You want the minimum diagnostic set that de‑risks implementation.
Inputs needed
  • Decision focus (reuse, retrofit, residuals, compliance, brine) and timeline/decision deadline.
  • Constraints (space, shutdown periods, utilities, operator capacity, approvals).
  • Data available (sampling, SCADA logs, drawings, O&M history) and data gaps.
Deliverables
  • A bounded scope statement: objective + performance requirements + what must be proven.
  • A routed diagnostic plan (minimum tools first) and a short data checklist.
  • A ‘what vendors must assume’ note to prevent scope drift in quotes.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Starting technology conversations before setting the objective and performance requirements.
  • Letting verification and monitoring expectations be implied instead of defined.
KPIs & verification requirements
Target capability level (e.g., reuse class / compliance margin) and verification approach (what can be measured). Data completeness score and uncertainty flags. Coordination clarity score (connections, bypass, residuals responsibilities).

Smart Intakes - Start here · Data coverage · vendor-ready
Technical Intake
Structure inputs for flows, loads, inlet characteristics, duty cycles and connections.
+
Use when
  • You need an inputs pack that vendors can price reliably (and comparably).
  • The site has variability and you must define the operating range beyond averages.
  • Retrofit connections and utility constraints must be made explicit.
Inputs needed
  • Flows (avg/peak), load ranges, duty cycles, shock events, and seasonal variation notes.
  • Quality drivers and constraints (TSS/COD/FOG/surfactants, metals/salinity if relevant).
  • Hydraulics/as‑builts, utilities, access and connections, operations and maintenance constraints.
Deliverables
  • Vendor-ready inputs pack + data list + assumptions note for missing data.
  • Operating range limits (must‑handle events) and what must be monitored.
  • A shortlist of data closures (sampling, trend pulls, inspections) to reduce risk fastest.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Issuing specs based on one‑off samples or averages.
  • Leaving connection and utility constraints implicit - vendors then price different scopes.
KPIs & verification requirements
Data coverage (% of operating range represented), variability ranges, confidence grading. Connection feasibility flags and utility readiness checks. Monitoring feasibility (what can be measured vs what would be assumed).

Process Health & Retrofit · Process stability & drift review
System Healthcheck
Rapid triage of capacity, compliance and operability issues with a retrofit view.
+
Use when
  • Effluent drift, process instability, rising energy or recurrent alarms are observed.
  • You need quick triage before committing to a major retrofit scope.
  • You suspect constraints are operational/control‑related rather than ‘more equipment’.
Inputs needed
  • Recent performance trends (SCADA/alarms), lab history, energy profile, and maintenance backlog.
  • Process configuration, setpoints, operator routines, and any recent changes.
  • Hydraulics/layout constraints and known pinch points (filters, aeration, solids handling).
Deliverables
  • Failure‑mode list (root causes) and ranked corrective actions (quick wins vs capital items).
  • Retrofit options with control/monitoring implications and coordination notes.
  • A short verification plan (what to trend/measure to confirm improvements).
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Treating symptoms (e.g., chemical over‑dosing) instead of root causes.
  • Missing control loop / instrumentation issues that drive instability.
KPIs & verification requirements
Compliance pass rate, alarm frequency, energy intensity, downtime/bypass events. Throughput vs capacity limits, headloss trends, backwash/cleaning frequency. Solids handling stability indicators (cake %TS variability, haulage frequency).

Process Health & Retrofit · Capacity/compliance upgrade planning
Retrofit Readiness Tool
De-risk connections and phasing; check footprint and utilities early.
+
Use when
  • Capacity/compliance upgrades are planned under tight footprint and downtime constraints.
  • You need to define what can be retained vs what must be replaced - and in what sequence.
  • Connections, bypass strategy, and construction phasing are the main risks.
Inputs needed
  • As‑builts/hydraulics, connection points, outage periods, bypass options and temporary works feasibility.
  • Utilities readiness (power, air, chemical storage, drains), access and lifting constraints.
  • Odour/off‑gas points and any regulatory/permit constraints affecting construction.
Deliverables
  • Phased retrofit plan + sequencing note (what happens when, and how compliance is protected).
  • Coordination map (connections, bypass, temporary works) and ‘must‑protect’ performance requirements.
  • Risk register items that must be closed before procurement.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • No bypass plan / temporary works assumptions left to vendors.
  • Utilities not checked early - causes late scope changes and cost creep.
KPIs & verification requirements
Outage feasibility (days), bypass exposure risk, tie‑in complexity score. Utility readiness score, access/maintenance clearance flags. Commissioning performance checkpoints per stage.

Process Health & Retrofit · Throughput & failure-mode optimization
Bottleneck Checklist
Find quick-win process constraints and control gaps.
+
Use when
  • The plant is broadly stable but throughput is constrained or micro‑failures recur.
  • You want targeted upgrades (controls, hydraulics, filtration) rather than a full rebuild.
  • You need a ranked list of ‘what limits performance’ with verification checks.
Inputs needed
  • Mass balance, SCADA trends, blower/pump curves, headloss/backwash records.
  • Operator logs (where pain occurs), maintenance history and recurring alarms.
  • Quality/flow variability notes and any critical shutdown and turnaround periods.
Deliverables
  • Ranked bottleneck list with corrective actions and expected benefit mechanisms.
  • Control/monitoring tweaks that improve stability (setpoints, alarms, sequencing).
  • Shortlist of upgrades with minimal coordination disruption.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Chasing a single bottleneck while ignoring upstream/downstream interactions.
  • Assuming equipment change fixes what is actually a control/operability issue.
KPIs & verification requirements
Throughput uplift potential, headloss reduction, energy intensity change. Backwash/cleaning frequency reduction, alarm rate reduction. Compliance pass rate stability (less variance).

Process Health & Retrofit · Variability & shock load control
Industrial Effluent Risk Diagnostic
Identify contributors and variability that break treatment stability and reuse plans.
+
Use when
  • Industrial contributors, cleaning cycles or shock loads drive instability/non‑compliance.
  • You need to protect biological stability and downstream reuse modules.
  • You suspect inhibitors, surfactants, FOG, metals or salinity spikes are present.
Inputs needed
  • Source inventory (contributors), variability pattern, equalisation and segregation conditions.
  • Pollutant fingerprint: organics profile, surfactants, FOG, metals, salinity (as relevant).
  • Event logs: batch dumps, CIP discharges, tanker discharges, storm flows.
Deliverables
  • Source risk map + recommended controls (segregation, equalisation, batch treatment triggers).
  • Pretreatment intent and monitoring points to protect the main process line.
  • A ‘must-handle events range for vendor specs.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Treating variability as an end‑of‑pipe polishing problem.
  • Assuming RO fixes everything; missing upstream control and segregation.
KPIs & verification requirements
Variability indices, inhibitor markers, equalisation adequacy, event frequency. Pass/fail triggers for pretreatment diversion, recovery time after shocks. Downstream protection indicators (SDI, toxicity proxies, surfactant markers).

Specialist Diagnostics · Source mapping · measurement plan · control routing
Odour & H₂S Diagnostic
Locate odour and H₂S generation/release points across liquid and solids handling, then define the most practical control route (prevent, capture, polish) with verification requirements.
+
Use when
  • Odour complaints occur (intermittent or persistent) and you need a clear source picture.
  • H₂S is suspected at headworks, wet wells, equalisation, sludge handling, or solids storage/transfer points.
  • Corrosion and safety risk are rising (confined spaces, ventilation points, near-field releases).
  • You need to choose between dosing, capture & treatment, or upstream process changes - and avoid over-building.
Inputs needed
  • Site layout + ventilation/capture points; wet-well/headworks configuration; sludge/solids routing and storage details.
  • Operator/event notes: when/where odour occurs; weather/rainfall patterns; maintenance and cleaning cycles.
  • Any available data: DO/ORP, sulphide (or surrogate), pH, temperature, ammonia, COD, flow patterns and retention times.
  • If available: spot H₂S readings, complaint log, corrosion observations, confined-space monitoring procedures.
Deliverables
  • Odour/H₂S source map (generation vs release points) across liquid and solids lines.
  • Measurement plan (what to test, where, and at what cadence) aligned to access and safety constraints.
  • Control-route shortlist: prevent/suppress (liquid), capture/treat (air), and solids-handling controls - with duty assumptions made explicit.
  • Vendor scope basis: boundaries, duty points, seasonal patterns, and performance and verification requirements.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Treating odour as only an air-treatment problem (often starts with liquid conditions and residence time).
  • Measuring at the wrong points/times and missing episodic peaks (storm events, shutdowns, cleaning cycles).
  • Jumping to scrubbers/biofilters before confirming source strength, duty cycle and capture integrity.
  • Ignoring the solids line (dewatering, storage, blending) where odour can dominate.
KPIs & verification requirements
Defines the indicator set and monitoring cadence needed to confirm improvements (peaks, duration, event frequency, response to interventions).

Specialist Diagnostics · Upstream segregation & risk control
Source Control & Pretreatment
Upstream segregation, oil/grease, heavy metals and surfactants - to protect the core process line and reuse plan.
+
Use when
  • Non‑domestic inputs drive instability or you need upstream protection for MBBR+/Micra+/ROCore+.
  • You suspect FOG/surfactants/metals/inhibitors or uncontrolled batch dumps.
  • You want clear triggers for diversion/batch treatment and monitoring points.
Inputs needed
  • Source inventory and discharge patterns; equalisation/segregation options.
  • Pollutant fingerprint and variability (metals/organics/surfactants/FOG/salinity as relevant).
  • Current monitoring capability (grab vs composite vs online) and response constraints.
Deliverables
  • Source control plan + pretreatment intent + trigger framework (what to divert, when).
  • Monitoring points (what to measure) and response actions aligned to operability.
  • Coordination notes for vendor specs (what upstream controls are assumed).
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Forcing downstream modules onto uncontrolled feeds.
  • No trigger framework - operators cannot act consistently during events.
KPIs & verification requirements
Trigger compliance (% events correctly captured), variability reduction indicators. Downstream protection indicators (SDI/scaling indices, toxicity proxies, surfactant markers). Equalisation adequacy and event capture rate.

Specialist Diagnostics · Scaling/fouling root-cause & pretreatment
Pretreatment Diagnostic
Confirm the upstream conditioning route that protects downstream modules.
+
Use when
  • Scaling/fouling or contaminant risk threatens downstream performance and OPEX.
  • You need to confirm the upstream conditioning route before committing to RO/membranes/high‑rate polishing.
  • You want clear ‘do / don’t’ rules for feeds that should bypass RO.
Inputs needed
  • Chemistry split (organics vs metals), salinity/TDS, scaling/fouling indices, particle load.
  • Variability patterns and event discharges; equalisation and segregation conditions.
  • Existing pretreatment (screens/DAF/chem), chemical history and performance issues.
Deliverables
  • Pretreatment pathway: what must be removed/conditioned and where (and why).
  • RO‑suitability decision logic (what should go to RO vs bypass to alternative routes).
  • Monitoring and performance requirements to carry into ToR and commissioning.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Assuming RO solves non‑RO‑able feeds; ignoring upstream control.
  • Chasing symptoms (fouling) without resolving root cause drivers.
KPIs & verification requirements
SDI/turbidity proxies, scaling indices, residual oxidant compatibility indicators. Removal targets for key drivers (metals/FOG/surfactants/organics). Stability indicators (variance reduction, event capture rate).

Specialist Diagnostics · Heat integration & energy recovery
Thermal Integration Tool
Capture heat synergies that shift OPEX and stability.
+
Use when
  • Heat can shift feasibility/OPEX (drying, digestion conditioning, pasteurisation, heat recovery).
  • Thermal options are being considered and you need realistic duty-cycle and fouling assumptions.
  • You need integration notes for controls, materials, and scaling risk.
Inputs needed
  • Heat sources/sinks, temperatures, duty cycles, materials limits and fouling history.

Deliverables

  • OPEX impact factors (energy intensity, heat recovered, pumping penalties).

Pitfalls to avoid
  • Ignoring scaling/material compatibility; underestimating cleaning burden.
  • No control strategy - thermal systems drift and lose performance.
KPIs & verification requirements
Heat recovered (kW), ΔT approach, fouling rate proxy, energy offset. System availability impact and maintenance burden indicators. Cost per unit heat delivered/recovered (optional).

Specialist Diagnostics · Brine readiness & solids conditions
Industrial Brine & ZLD
TDS/TSS split, metals/organics split, heat source and energy KPIs - to confirm if post-RO ZLD is viable.
+
Use when
  • Brine disposal constraints exist and you need a credible post‑RO / non‑RO brine route.
  • You need to separate brine character (high organics vs high metals) and decide RO vs bypass.
  • Heat source availability and solids handling are major viability factors.
Inputs needed
  • Brine source(s) and chemistry split (organics vs metals), scaling/fouling indices, solids conditions.
  • Heat availability (temperature, continuity), utilities and footprint constraints.

Deliverables
  • ZLD readiness view: what can go to RO vs what must bypass to thermal/brine management.
  • Viability flags: heat/energy intensity, solids handling and coordination responsibilities.
  • ToR-ready assumptions and KPIs for vendor comparability.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Assuming all brine is RO reject; ignoring non‑RO‑able feeds.
  • Skipping solids conditions checks and heat strategy - leads to unimplementable scope.
KPIs & verification requirements
TDS, scaling/fouling indices, energy intensity indicators (kWh/m³ brine). Solids spec and handling losses, availability assumptions. Recovery split (RO vs thermal segment) and disposal residual fraction.

Recovery yield allowances, handling loss allowances, coordination response times. Ash quality consistency indicators and storage loss proxies. Throughput/availability assumptions for the ash recovery segment.

Reuse Planner
Map water types to reuse classes and identify the barrier intent that fits the target.
+

Net recovery (%), reuse fraction, demand met (%), storage turnover days. Key performance indicators by end‑use (turbidity, E. coli proxy, residual control as relevant). Operational stability indicators (alarm rate, cleaning frequency).

Strategic Screens · Impact & verification readiness
ESG Priority Screening
Rank opportunities by impact and verification readiness - without turning it into a glossy ESG story.
+
Use when
  • You need to prioritise investments across water, energy and residuals with verification readiness.
  • Stakeholders want impact, but delivery practicality must govern selection.
  • You need a decision-ready shortlist with measurable indicators (not glossy storyline).
Inputs needed
  • Site priorities, constraints, stakeholder drivers, and data available.
  • Possible opportunities (reuse, recovery, energy, residuals) and delivery complexity notes.
  • Verification requirements: what must be measured at commissioning and in steady state.
Deliverables

Water Quality Requirements & Treatment Fit
Define the target water quality (by end‑use/permit) and translate it into measurable ranges, verification requirements, and a clear treatment basis.
+
Use when
  • End‑use is clear (irrigation/cooling/process) but target quality is not - risk of over‑ or under‑specifying.
  • Reuse assurance or permitting needs a traceable parameter set and monitoring approach.
  • You want vendors to price/guarantee performance against a consistent performance basis.
Inputs needed
  • Intended end‑uses, storage/demand pattern, and any mixing/blending realities.
  • Local guidance/permit constraints (or a shortlist of likely standards) and performance approach (minimum vs ‘assurance’).
  • Influent variability data (sampling snapshots, logs) and any known problem constituents.
Deliverables
  • Requirement matrix: parameters + target ranges + measurement method/frequency (clearly bounded).
  • Treatment fit shortlist: plausible paths by module family, with ‘what each stage controls’ notes.
  • Verification & performance requirements: what must be verified at commissioning and what must be monitored in operation.
Pitfalls to avoid
  • Copy‑pasting another site’s spec without matching end‑use risk, measurement method, and local performance rules.
  • Mixing compliance targets and operational targets without stating which is which (and what happens in excursions).
  • Letting vendors choose the boundary (parameters, units, averaging periods) after pricing.
KPIs & verification requirements
Target ranges (e.g., TSS/turbidity, microbiological indicators/log reduction, residual disinfectant where relevant). Salinity/scaling indicators (EC/TDS, hardness/silica where relevant) aligned to end‑use risk. Assurance approach score: metered vs estimated coverage + sampling/monitoring completeness.

+
